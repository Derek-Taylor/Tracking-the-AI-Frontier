# Tracking the Frontier

## Who Is Driving the Frontier, How Fast It Is Moving, and Why Old Assumptions Fail

**Time Target:** 5–7 minutes total

## Section Purpose

This section should:

* define what is meant by the AI frontier,
* identify the major frontier labs,
* establish the pace of progress over the last 36 months,
* and explain why outdated mental models of AI are no longer sufficient.

This section is not meant to be exhaustive. Its job is to create urgency and context quickly so the audience is ready to interpret the demonstrations that follow.

By the end of this section, the audience should think:

> “The frontier is moving fast enough that I cannot rely on my impression of AI from a year or two ago.”

## 1. Defining the Frontier

### What Do We Mean by the AI Frontier?

**Purpose**
Give the audience a concise definition of the frontier so the term feels meaningful and grounded rather than vague.

**Core Message**
The AI frontier refers to the leading edge of model capability and tool development: the systems, labs, and products pushing what these models can do in reasoning, multimodality, autonomy, and usability.

**Key Points**

* The frontier is the current leading edge of capability
* It is shaped by both underlying models and the products built on top of them
* The frontier matters because it often becomes the mainstream surprisingly quickly
* The educational implications tend to arrive after the capabilities, not before them

**Presentation Materials**
A good visual here would be:

* a simple slide with the phrase **The AI Frontier** in the center
* surrounded by terms like:

  * reasoning
  * multimodality
  * coding
  * long context
  * tool use
  * autonomy
  * usability


**Speaker Function**
This should give the term precision without becoming overly technical.

**Transition**
“So who is actually driving that leading edge right now?”

## 2. The Major Frontier Labs

### Who Is Driving the Frontier?

**Purpose**
Introduce the major organizations shaping the current state of AI without turning this into an industry history lesson.

**Core Message**
A relatively small number of frontier labs are driving much of the rapid visible progress in AI, and the competition among them is accelerating both capability growth and product deployment.

**Key Points**

* OpenAI
* Google / DeepMind
* Anthropic
* Other significant players may be acknowledged briefly, but the focus should remain narrow and practical
* These labs are not only improving raw model performance; they are shaping how the public encounters AI through products and interfaces

**Presentation Materials**
A good visual here would be:

* a clean slide with the names/logos of the major labs
* optionally paired with a short phrase under each:

  * OpenAI — broad public adoption and productization
  * Google / DeepMind — multimodal ecosystem and research depth
  * Anthropic — strong positioning around reasoning, safety, and professional use

Keep this slide visually restrained.

**Speaker Function**
The audience does not need a deep corporate breakdown. They just need to understand that this movement is being driven by a handful of highly resourced labs pushing quickly and competitively.

**Transition**
“The more important point than who the labs are is what has happened over a very short span of time.”

## 3. The Pace of Progress

### Why the Last 36 Months Matter

**Purpose**
Show that the pace of change has been unusually fast and visible.

**Core Message**
The last 36 months have brought rapid improvement in capability, usability, multimodality, and workflow integration. The result is that many people’s mental model of AI is already out of date.

**Key Points**

* Improvement has not been limited to better chatbot responses
* Major visible gains have occurred in:

  * reasoning
  * coding assistance
  * multimodal input/output
  * long-context processing
  * workflow automation
  * polished product integration
* Capabilities that seemed surprising recently can become normal very quickly
* What matters educationally is not just the current snapshot, but the rate of change

**Presentation Materials**
This section would benefit from one of the following:

#### Option A: Capability Timeline

A simple 2023 → 2024 → 2025 → 2026 timeline showing categories such as:

* better reasoning
* stronger multimodality
* more agentic workflows
* better coding support
* larger context windows
* more polished interfaces


## 4. Why Old Assumptions Fail

### The Danger of an Outdated Mental Model

**Purpose**
Translate the pace of progress into an educational problem.

**Core Message**
If educators are relying on assumptions formed in 2023 or even 2024, they may be underestimating what current tools can do and misjudging what students and teachers now need to be prepared for.

**Key Points**

* Old assumptions can lead to weak policy
* They can also lead to weak assignment design
* They may cause educators to underestimate both the promise and the disruption of current tools
* The problem is not just misunderstanding one product; it is misunderstanding the trajectory of the whole landscape
* Schools tend to update more slowly than the technology itself

**Presentation Materials**
A strong visual here could be:

* a slide titled **Old Assumptions That No Longer Hold**

Potential examples:

* “AI can only do shallow work”
* “AI outputs are always obviously bad”
* “Students will only use AI for cheating”

This is a good place for a clean, minimal slide with 3 assumptions you plan to challenge during the demos.

**Speaker Function**
This is the hinge of the section. It should create readiness for the hands-on examples.

**Transition**
“To make that concrete, I want to show two examples of frontier implementations that are especially relevant to educators.”
